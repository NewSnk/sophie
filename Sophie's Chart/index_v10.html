2020 U.S. Textile Imports by Type

To get a sense for how the textile trade in the U.S. was effected by COVID-19, we start by examining cotton imports.

Cotton imports in the U.S. fell below the ten year annual average during the early part of 2020.

Relative to the ten-year historical average total import is the shortfall represents a reduction of over 14%, centered around the early part of the year (January through July).
By the end of 2020, trade had recovered, and the U.S. had returned to a trading surplus relative to the monthly ten-year average.

To account for seasonality in the textile trade, and to establish firmer correlation to COVID-19 related events in early 2020, we net out the trading surplus from the shortfall heuristically.

This leaves us with a surplus-adjusted monthly shortfall value that may be used to analyze differences between 2020, and the previous ten years in U.S. trade history.
We can see that the COVID restrictions timeline in early 2020 correlate quite closely with the shape of trade deficits

While the early part of 2020 is less clear, it becomes visually apparent that during the early stages of 2020, when states were starting to take up quarantine and determine restrictions, the cotton import trade started to suffer.

By late March when most U.S. states started to easy restrictions (to some degree), trade appeared to start the return to normal, reaching to ten-year normals by July.
